The starting point for my individual assignment is an untested hypothesis based on my market observations in the my time as trader and some of the backtest results of the group assignment. It is not a tested hyphotesis. It would be interesting to test, but would requires much more effort than available for this work.

My hypothesis starts in the observation that the tested strategies are always 100% invested, and are long-only, as such:
1. In prolonged bullish regimes a subset of asset classes shows strong upward trends. Volatility-minimizing allocations, however, tilt toward uncorrelated or lower-volatility assets (and sometimes assets with weak or negative returns), so they systematically forgo maximum upside capture.

2. During economic or social disturbances overall volatility rises. Safe-haven assets (e.g., gold, bonds) may become relatively bullish, but their trends are typically weaker than the equity uptrends they replace.

3. Market crashes are often rapid and highly correlated across asset classes. Because these declines are quick, rules-based volatility/minimization strategies cannot rebalance fast enough to avoid large losses.

4. Conclusion: the group-assignment strategies are likely to underperform in strong bull markets (missing upside) and remain vulnerable to fast, cross-asset crashes (insufficient tail protection).

We tried to allow shor selling in some of the strategies, but even that didn't improve the returns. In fact, the results were even worse.

With this in mind, for the individual assignment strategies that:
1. Could detect assets with bullish trends.
2. Can invest only in part of the assets with the strongest bullish trend and leave the rest untouched.
3. If there is no asset with bullish trend they invest in a risk-free asset.
4. Have the possibility to implement drawdown protections: stop loss and trailing stop loss.
